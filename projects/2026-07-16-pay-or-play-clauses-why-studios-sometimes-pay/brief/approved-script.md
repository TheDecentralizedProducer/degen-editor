# Brief: Pay-or-Play Clauses — Why Studios Sometimes Pay Stars Full Fee for Movies Never Made

## Scripts

### reel

Nicolas Cage was paid twenty million dollars for a movie he never filmed. Tim Burton spent months in pre-production — costume tests, concept art, full studio resources committed. The project was cancelled before a single day of principal photography. And the studio was still legally required to write Cage a check. That film was Superman Lives. And the mechanic behind that payment is called pay-or-play. Here's what most producers don't know about how this actually works. A pay-or-play commitment means the studio agrees to either produce the project or pay the attached talent their full contracted fee — regardless. The studio retains the right to kill the film. But if they exercise that right, they write the check. From the talent side, pay-or-play converts a speculative development attachment into a real financial commitment. An A-list director or a bankable actor won't hold months of their availability for a production that might never get greenlit — not without pay-or-play. It's what makes the schedule commitment binding on both sides. From the studio side, they're buying optionality: they control the talent's attachment and availability while retaining the right to kill the project — as long as they pay. Now, does every talent deal include pay-or-play? No. Leverage matters. A-list directors and stars get it. Most people don't. But here's the lesson. First: pay-or-play is a real financial liability from the moment you sign it — not from the day you get your greenlight. It has to be modeled in your development budget immediately. Second: not every attachment is a commitment. Until there's a pay-or-play clause, an attached name is a courtesy, not an obligation. Third: if you're building toward your first production deal, knowing when to ask for pay-or-play — and when you have the leverage to get it — is the difference between a real commitment and a placeholder.
