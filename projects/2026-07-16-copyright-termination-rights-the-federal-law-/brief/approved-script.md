## Scripts

### reel

Sylvester Stallone signed away Rocky in 1976. He was a first-time writer. He needed the deal. So he assigned the rights as part of the original production agreement. Rocky went on to win Best Picture and become one of the most valuable sports franchises in entertainment history. Decades later, Stallone got it back. Not through renegotiation. Not through litigation. Through a federal law that most creators who signed away IP rights don't know exists. Here's what most filmmakers and writers don't know about copyright termination. The Copyright Act of 1976 gives creators who assigned their work a legal right to reclaim that copyright — but the window to do it is narrow, the process is highly technical, and missing the deadline ends the right permanently. Here's how the timeline actually works. For any work assigned after January 1, 1978, you can file a termination notice during a five-year window that opens exactly 35 years after the original assignment date. If you signed away your screenplay in 1990, your window opened in 2025 and closes in 2030. Miss that window, and the studio owns your copyright permanently under the original deal terms — regardless of what the work became worth. Here's the legal lesson. First: termination rights don't activate automatically. You have to file a legally compliant termination notice within the window, or the right expires — the law does not protect you from missing the deadline. Second: works made-for-hire are not eligible for termination. The law specifically excludes them, which is why understanding how your original deal was structured is not a detail question — it's the threshold question. Third: studios have institutional resources to contest or delay reclamation. If you have standing to file, doing it without a copyright attorney is not advisable. If you're a filmmaker or writer who assigned rights in the 1980s or early 1990s, your termination window is either open right now or opening within a few years. Link in bio. Go read.
