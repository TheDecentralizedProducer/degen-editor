# Brief: Backend Points Dilution — Why 100 Points on Paper Often Add Up to More Than 100%

## Key facts

- Winston Groom held 3% net profit deal on Forrest Gump; film grossed over $675M; Paramount accounting showed no net profit
- Sing Sing (2023) gave entire crew backend participation — verifiable counter-model where points paid out

## Scripts

### reel

You negotiated 5 points of backend. Here's the problem: 100 points of net profit on paper often adds up to more than 100 percent once every stakeholder has their slice — and 5 percent of a number that's been contractually defined down to zero is still zero. Let me explain how backend dilution actually works. When a film gets financed and packaged, every stakeholder at the table wants backend participation. The studio takes points. The sales agent negotiates points. Key talent — director, lead actors, the writer — all get backend as part of their deals. Executive producers take points. Financiers take points. By the time the film is fully packaged, the total points in play routinely exceed 100 percent. That is not an error in the deal memo — it's a structure. Here's why it functions: net profit is defined contractually, not mathematically. Your distribution agreement specifies exactly which costs get deducted before net profit is calculated. If those definitions are written aggressively enough, net profit lands at zero regardless of total gross revenue. Winston Groom wrote the novel that became Forrest Gump and held a 3 percent net profit participation deal with Paramount. Forrest Gump grossed over $675 million at the box office. Paramount's accounting reportedly showed the film had not reached net profit under their contractual formula. Groom received almost nothing in backend. The counter-model worth studying is the 2023 film Sing Sing — which structured its deal so the entire crew, not just above-the-line talent, received backend participation points and it was tied to the amount of time worked on the project so it was weighted to the value of your input in the project. The film performed, and those points paid out. That structure exists and is achievable. It's just rare. The number of your backend points matters far less than where in the waterfall those points sit and how net profit is defined in your specific agreement. Ask for the definition, not just the percentage.
