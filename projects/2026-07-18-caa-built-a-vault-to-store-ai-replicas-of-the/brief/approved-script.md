I'm not gonna lie — when I first saw the Vanity Fair piece about CAA building an AI replica vault, I thought it was going to be another AI panic story. Turns out it's something way more dystopian. And more interesting. CAA has been building an archive of digital replicas of their clients. Likeness scans. Voice data. The whole works.  Performance captures. The infrastructure to license a client's AI double to a production without the actor needing to be present, available, or even on the same continent. Here's what everyone's missing about this. The 2023 SAG-AFTRA deal spent months hammering out AI provisions — consent requirements, session minimums, informed authorization before a production can use your digital likeness. That negotiation got wall-to-wall coverage. And it matters. But it governs what happens during a covered production. It doesn't govern what a talent agency can collect, store, and license under the representation agreement their clients signed years before those provisions existed. These are two separate legal instruments. And the agency agreement is the one nobody's been reading closely. Because here's the shift: CAA isn't positioning itself as a talent agency that happens to have AI tools. They're building the infrastructure of an IP licensing company. The vault isn't a feature — it's an inventory system. And if you're an actor whose data is in that system, the question of who controls licensing decisions, how revenue gets split, and what requires your per-deal consent is answered by a document you probably signed in a very different era. The agencies that move fastest on this infrastructure will have a structural advantage in how AI-era productions get cast, staffed, and budgeted. The actors whose representatives built that vault on their behalf may or may not have understood what they authorized. That gap — between what SAG negotiated and what the agency agreement actually says — is where the next fight is going to happen.
